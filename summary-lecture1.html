Lecture 1: Ethics and Computer Ethics

One-Page Summary

Definition of Ethics

Ethics is the theoretical examination of morality, concerned with principles of human duty and moral conduct. It encompasses the moral principles that guide individuals and groups, and the rules of conduct recognized in specific areas of human life.

Computer Ethics

James Moor (1985) defined computer ethics as addressing situations where:

  • Computer technology is essentially involved
  • There is uncertainty about what to do and how to understand the situation
  • Policy vacuums exist that require new thinking and new policies

Why Computer Ethics Matters

  • Technology is rapidly changing human existence
  • Tech developers (especially "Big Tech") often lack empathy and responsibility
  • Technology decisions impact civil dialogue, privacy, and fairness
  • Each new technology creates a moral vacuum that needs ethical consideration

Four Perspectives on Technology and Society

1. Technological Neutrality ("just a tool")

  • Technology viewed as a neutral tool like a hammer
  • Critique: Digital technologies make ethical decisions themselves
  • Autonomous technologies can plan, decide, and act with real-world consequences

2. Technological Determinism

  • Technology causes certain ways of doing things
  • Assumes one-directional relationship: technology â†’ society
  • Example: "What impact does Twitter have on political discourse?"

3. Social Determinism (Actor-Network Theory)

  • Human networks shape technology
  • No hierarchy in relationships between actors
  • Criticized for treating all elements equally and being more descriptive than explanatory

4. Social Shaping of Technology

  • Balanced approach between determinism and social theories
  • Technology affects society through:
    • Affordances: Makes tasks easier
    • Constraints: Makes some tasks harder
    • Preconditions: Limits on who can use technology and how
    • Unintended consequences: Unforeseen impacts

Power Abstraction and Distancing

Technology distances users from consequences of their actions. "Abstracted power" is human influence over systems that obscures consequences. Technological interfaces hide emotional impacts.

Two key factors contribute to distancing:

  • Technological Intermediation: Opacity between developers and users
  • Computational Thinking: Focus on abstractions rather than human impacts

Key Questions for Ethical Analysis

  • Who benefits from a particular technology?
  • Who suffers under it?
  • Whom might it empower?
  • Whom might it oppress?

Practical Ethics

Involves making decisions with future impacts, requires determining right/wrong in specific situations, addresses personal uncertainties and conflicts of opinion, and forms the foundation for ethical decision-making in technology.
