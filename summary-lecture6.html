Lecture 6: Datafication and Data Justice

One-Page Summary

Understanding Datafication

Datafication is the process of turning human behaviour and social activities into data points that can be collected and analysed (Cukier and Mayer-Schonberger, 2013). It involves rendering the social and natural world in machine-readable digital format (Williamson, 2020). This transformation has affected virtually every aspect of society, including education, healthcare, social interactions, and governance.

Datafication in Education

  • Learning analytics and AI to monitor, assess, and personalise learning experiences
  • Student engagement monitoring systems
  • Online learning systems (Moodle, Blackboard)
  • Robot/Chatbot teaching assistants
  • Interactive games and classroom response systems
  • Standardised assessments (PISA, TIMMS) and university rankings

Benefits of Datafication in Education

  • Personalised learning experiences
  • Data-driven strategies for improvement
  • Improved student outcomes
  • Enhanced measurement across education systems
  • Better-informed decision-making

Risks and Challenges of Datafication

Privacy Concerns

The anonymity spectrum ranges from anonymous grouped data (most private) to identifiable individualised data (least private). As data becomes more individualised and identifiable, privacy risks increase significantly.

Algorithmic Bias

Algorithms used to process datafied information can perpetuate or amplify existing biases, leading to unfair outcomes for certain groups.

Surveillance

Datafication enables unprecedented levels of monitoring, such as student attendance systems that track individuals' movements and activities.

Filter Bubbles and Echo Chambers

Algorithmic filtering can limit exposure to diverse perspectives, reinforcing existing beliefs and creating information silos.

Pedagogic Reductionism/Quantification Bias

The tendency to focus only on what can be measured, potentially overlooking important qualitative aspects of learning and human experience.

Data Justice

Data justice refers to fairness in the way people are made visible, represented, and treated as a result of their production of digital data (Linnet, 2017). It encompasses visibility, engagement with technology, and non-discrimination.

Six Pillars of Data Justice (GPAI, 2022)

  1. Power: Understanding how power operates in data collection and use, and addressing power imbalances that shape the distribution of benefits and risks
  2. Equity: Confronting root causes of data injustices and interrogating choices about data acquisition and use, particularly for marginalized populations
  3. Access: Ensuring equitable access to the benefits of data processing through distributive, capabilities-centered, restorative, and representational justice
  4. Identity: Addressing the social character of data and problematising its construction and categorisation, which is shaped by sociocultural conditions and historical contexts
  5. Participation: Promoting democratisation of data practices by involving impacted communities, policymakers, practitioners, and developers in collaborative decision-making
  6. Knowledge: Recognising that diverse forms of knowledge can provide valuable insights into the impacts of data-intensive innovation

Requirements for Human Flourishing in a Datafied Society

  • Accessible, stable, and trustworthy infrastructure
  • Supportive legal and regulatory framework for secure online interactions
  • Informed and knowledgeable understanding for all stakeholders of the technologies in place and how they might be used

Responsibilities of Data System Designers and Developers

  • Interrogate and critique power imbalances in data innovation
  • Focus on the transformative power of data equity
  • Ensure responsible data sharing practices
  • Challenge harmful categorisations and ensure accurate representation
  • Promote inclusive participation that doesn't reinforce existing power imbalances
  • Value non-technical, socially-situated knowledge in data work
