Lecture 8: Computer & Related Crimes

One-Page Summary

Understanding Cybercrime in the UK Legal Context

Cybercrime refers to offenses involving the use of computers or networks. These are categorized into two broad types: computer-enabled crimes (traditional crimes facilitated by computers) and computer-dependent crimes (crimes that can only be committed using computers). UK legislation addresses both categories through targeted legal instruments, with common cybercrimes including hacking, malware dissemination, identity theft, and online fraud.

Types of Cybercrimes and Legal Frameworks

Hacking and Unauthorized Access

Defined under Section 1 of the Computer Misuse Act 1990, this involves intentional unauthorized access to computer systems or data. It does not require data theft or system damage to constitute an offense and is considered a summary offense but can escalate based on intent or outcome.

Denial-of-Service (DoS) Attacks

Covered under Section 3 of the Computer Misuse Act 1990, these constitute unauthorized acts intended to impair the operation of a computer. This includes overwhelming servers or networks to disrupt access or services, often targeting public institutions, financial systems, or critical infrastructure.

Malware and System Interference

Prosecuted under Section 3 of the Computer Misuse Act 1990, this involves deploying software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized control. Criminal liability arises even if no actual harm occurs, provided intent is proven.

Phishing and Online Fraud

Typically prosecuted under the Fraud Act 2006, this involves deception to acquire sensitive data such as credentials or financial information. Section 2 (Fraud by False Representation) is most often invoked.

Identity Theft and Identity Fraud

Governed under the Fraud Act 2006, particularly Sections 2 and 4. Identity theft involves unlawful acquisition of personal information, while identity fraud refers to using that information to commit further crimes.

Key Cyber and Networking Laws in the UK

  • Computer Misuse Act 1990: Addresses unauthorized access and digital sabotage. Established three primary offenses: unauthorized access, access with intent, and unauthorized modification.
  • Communications Act 2003: Regulates offensive use of public communication networks. Section 127 criminalizes offensive, indecent, obscene, or menacing messages.
  • Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 (RIPA): Regulates surveillance and interception of communications, allowing public bodies to access communications data under specific conditions.
  • Telecommunications Regulations: Enable employers to monitor calls, emails, and internet use for specific purposes, such as preventing crime and ensuring compliance with regulatory practices.
  • Data Retention and Investigatory Powers Act (DRIPA) 2014: Introduced emergency powers for data retention following legal challenges to EU directives.
  • Telecommunications (Security) Act 2021: Enhances the cyber resilience of UK telecoms infrastructure by imposing duties on providers to identify, assess, and mitigate security risks.

Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) Laws

The ICO is the UK's independent authority for upholding information rights, enforcing multiple laws related to data privacy, security, and transparency. Its jurisdiction spans both public and private sectors.

Key ICO-Enforced Laws

  • Data Protection Act 2018: UK's core personal data legislation, implementing and tailoring GDPR to the UK context.
  • General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR): Establishes seven key principles for data processing, enhances data subject rights, and imposes stricter obligations on data controllers and processors.
  • Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations (PECR) 2003: Regulates marketing emails, texts, cookies, and use of traffic and location data.
  • Freedom of Information Act 2000 (FOIA): Grants public access to information held by public authorities in the UK.
  • Environmental Information Regulations 2004 (EIR): Provides public access to environmental information held by public authorities.
  • Investigatory Powers Act 2016 (IPA): Authorizes interception and retention of communications data under strict conditions.

Network and Information Systems (NIS) Regulations 2018

Derived from EU Directive 2016/1148, these regulations aim to improve the security of network and information systems for essential services and digital providers. They apply to both public and private sector operators.

Key Components of NIS Regulations

  • Operators of Essential Services (OES): Organizations critical to national infrastructure and public welfare, including energy, transport, health, water, and digital infrastructure sectors.
  • Relevant Digital Service Providers (RDSPs): Include online marketplaces, search engines, and cloud computing services.
